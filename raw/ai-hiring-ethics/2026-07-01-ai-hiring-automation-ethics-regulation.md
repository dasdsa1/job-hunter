## Regulatory landscape

Colorado's AI Act (effective June 2026) and California's ADS Regulations (effective
October 2025) require employers to exercise "reasonable care" by incorporating meaningful
human review into AI-driven hiring processes. New York City law requires employers to
audit any AI hiring/promotion decision tool before using it, and to notify candidates at
least ten business days before such tools are used.

## Key ethical concerns

**Bias and discrimination:** AI systems learn from data; if that data reflects human
bias, algorithms can perpetuate or worsen it. Derek Mobley v. Workday: claimed Workday's
AI-powered screening tools disproportionately rejected older, Black, and disabled
applicants. Court certified a class of applicants over 40 under the ADEA — signals courts
are increasingly willing to hold AI vendors accountable as "agents" of the employer, not
just neutral tool providers.

**Transparency/accountability:** When AI makes hiring-adjacent decisions, it's unclear
whose job it is to adhere to ethical norms/labor law — compounded when the AI is
third-party vendor software, not built in-house.

## Best practices for ethical implementation

- Run pilot programs with diverse participants before full deployment, evaluating
  real-world tool performance against human benchmarks.
- Manual review option required for candidates with disabilities or non-native English
  speakers when AI tools have known accuracy limitations.
- Rigorously test for bias before deployment, continuously monitor post-deployment.
- Establish ethics committees, decision hierarchies, risk management protocols early.
- Be transparent with candidates about the extent AI/automation is used and how their
  data is used; commit against using collected data for unrelated/malicious purposes.
- Human retains the final call — hiring managers look at context, not just predictions.
  Job applicants should receive human interaction at pivotal points.
